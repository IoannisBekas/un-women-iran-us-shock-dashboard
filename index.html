How to read this dashboard

Screening tool, not a causal estimate

The score ranks exposure to plausible escalation pathways. It does not estimate how many women or girls became food insecure after the shock, because the current public data pull does not include sex- and age-disaggregated food-security outcomes.

Three-point read

Prices rose most in the Horn and East Africa

Matched same-month food-price increases peak in Sudan, Ethiopia, South Sudan and Somalia.

The ranking is broader than prices

The exposure score combines prices with IPC severity, displacement, assistance, import and gender-vulnerability pathways.

The evidence supports prioritization, not attribution

The analytical model can flag pressure and readiness. It cannot estimate a causal women/girls food-security impact.

For country-level filters and scenario exploration, open the country lens. For the full calculation logic and caveats, open the methodology page. For definitions, open the dashboard dictionary.

Before vs after

Food prices rose most sharply in Sudan, Ethiopia, South Sudan and Somalia

The source price panel compares like-for-like WFP/HDX market and commodity price records in post-shock 2026 months with the same calendar month in 2025, then keeps the highest positive observed increase per country. Flat, negative and unavailable countries remain visible so readers can distinguish a price rise from a flat comparison or a missing price comparison.
