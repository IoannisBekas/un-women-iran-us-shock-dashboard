How to read this dashboard

Screening tool, not a causal estimate

The score ranks exposure to plausible escalation pathways. It does not estimate how many women or girls became food insecure after the shock, because the current public data pull does not include sex- and age-disaggregated food-security outcomes.

Three-point read

Prices rose most in the Horn and East Africa

Matched same-month food-price increases peak in Sudan, Ethiopia, South Sudan and Somalia.

The ranking is broader than prices

The exposure score combines prices with IPC severity, displacement, assistance, import and gender-vulnerability pathways.

The evidence supports prioritization, not attribution

The analytical model can flag pressure and readiness. It cannot estimate a causal women/girls food-security impact.

Before vs after

Food prices rose most sharply in Sudan, Ethiopia, South Sudan and Somalia

The food-price comparison input compares like-for-like WFP/HDX market and commodity price records in post-shock 2026 months with the same calendar month in 2025, then keeps the highest positive observed increase per country. Flat, negative and unavailable countries remain visible so readers can distinguish a price rise from a flat comparison or a missing price comparison. The country evidence keeps peak increase and IPC latest timing together where public records allow comparison.

    Shock Exposure Index ranking

    Sudan and South Sudan top the exposure-priority ranking

    Scores combine price pressure, import exposure, agriculture inputs, IPC severity, displacement, assistance pressure and gender vulnerability.

    0-100 exposure score

    Higher bars mean higher relative exposure across the weighted model pathways. The 0-100 score supports prioritization; it is not a probability, forecast or measured causal impact.

    Calculated: 100 x sum(component_score x component_weight) across price, import, agriculture, IPC, WFP outcome, displacement, assistance and gender-vulnerability pathways.

    How to read: the ranking is a screening order for attention and monitoring, not an estimate of how much food security changed.

    Uses fields: countries[].score, components.*, indexWeights. Open Country Lens for the weighted decomposition.

    Data-confidence flags identify countries where missing or neutral-imputed public inputs make interpretation more cautious; a flag is not evidence of lower risk.

      Gender-sensitive lens

      Sudan and South Sudan top the gender-proxy priority list

      Proxy score

      Higher bars indicate stronger structural gender-vulnerability signals in the proxy inputs. This is a prioritization lens, not a measured before/after outcome for women or girls.

      Calculated: gender proxy priority combines the official exposure score with structural gender vulnerability, IPC severity, WFP outcomes, displacement, assistance, food-price, import and agriculture/fertilizer signals.

      How to read: use the ranking to decide where UN Women should look more closely, not to claim measured differential impact.

      Uses fields: genderProxy.score and selected components.*. See the decomposition below for contribution points.

      Contribution points

      Contribution points deconstruct the gender-sensitive proxy score. They explain the arithmetic behind prioritization; they are not measured SADD outcomes or causal gender impacts.

      Calculated: each row is input score x gender-proxy coefficient; 0-1 component inputs are converted to 0-100 contribution points.

      How to read: the largest rows are the inputs doing most of the arithmetic work for the selected country's proxy priority score.

      Uses fields: score, genderProxy.score, components.*. Full caveats are in the gender methodology.

      Two displacement signals are used

      The legacy proxy and the IDMC/IOM internal-displacement layer answer different questions. Read them side by side.
